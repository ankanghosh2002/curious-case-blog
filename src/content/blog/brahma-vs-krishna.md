Once, Lord Brahma visited Vrindavan to witness the eighth incarnation of Lord Vishnu—Lord Krishna. Curious to know the extent of Krishna's divine powers, Brahma decided to put Him to the test.

One day, while Krishna was in the forest with His friends and their cattle, He stepped away for a few moments. Seizing the opportunity, Brahma used his divine powers to take away all the cowherd boys and the cattle, hiding them safely in Brahmaloka. Satisfied with his plan, he returned to see how Krishna would react.

To Brahma, this entire act took only a fraction of a second. However, because time flows differently across different realms, the equivalent of several human years had passed on Earth.

When Brahma returned to Vrindavan, he was left speechless. The same boys and cattle were still there, happily playing and grazing as if nothing had ever happened. Yet Brahma knew they were also hidden in Brahmaloka. Unable to comprehend how they could exist in both places at once, he was overwhelmed with confusion.

Realizing that he was witnessing something beyond his understanding, Brahma approached Krishna with folded hands and said,

*"Forgive me, Almighty. Please reveal the truth."*

Krishna smiled and asked Brahma to close his eyes. When Brahma opened them again, he no longer saw the young cowherd of Vrindavan. Instead, he beheld the magnificent form of Lord Vishnu seated upon the eternal Shesh Nag, radiating infinite brilliance.

Lord Vishnu then revealed the truth.

He explained that during the entire period in which the original boys and cattle remained hidden, Krishna Himself had assumed the form of every single child and every single calf. He had lived among their families exactly as they would have, ensuring that no one experienced their absence. Every smile, every conversation, every moment of their lives had been Krishna Himself.

Vishnu then explained the vast difference between human time and Brahma's time, revealing the unimaginable scale of the universe and the countless worlds that exist within His creation.

He told Brahma that a lifespan of one hundred Brahma years is merely the duration of a single exhalation of Lord Vishnu. At the end of every Brahma's life, another Brahma is born to begin the cycle of creation once again.

Finally, Vishnu said,

> *"I am the **Supreme Power** over all Brahmas."*

This story carries a profound philosophical message about the nature of time, creation, and the supremacy of the Divine. It also presents a fascinating parallel to the Theory of Relativity proposed by Professor Albert Einstein in 1905. While the story is rooted in Hindu mythology and Einstein's theory is based on modern physics, both describe a universe in which time is not absolute but flows differently depending on the observer's frame of reference. This remarkable similarity has inspired many to draw philosophical connections between ancient wisdom and modern scientific thought.
