Arrange marriage is a prostitution pit in which society pushes their offspring to drown and eventually survive by the key principle of *'habit'*. When I dare say *'prostitution'*, it’s from both sides. It is like a corporate contract which ensures wealth, stability, family status, and physical appearance, and can often be noted as a deal of life, where the woman is expected to provide domestic labor and a progeny or several progenies, and the man is alleged to provide family security, status, and money to the home. Now many people oppose this by saying:
*“You two can fall in love after marriage.”* But this statement is the most nonsensical belief of this age, because if we lock up two people and say:
*“You two are supposed to be together, continue the bloodline, and maintain a family esteem higher than before.”*
...then they are bound to fall in love.*It’s like Stockholm syndrome, which is basically a psychological response in which a victim of abuse develops an emotional attachment or sympathy toward their captor or abuser.* Again, this is valid from both sides; the women are trapped the same way the men are. But this is pure attachment born of habit, not love.

![arrange marriage is prostitution](/uploads/isarrangemarriageintitutionalizedprostitution.jpg)

Lately, I got into Osho’s writing and sayings, and it has triggered me to believe that society dictates that babies born before marriage are bastards or illegitimate, but Osho says the babies who are conceived through arranged marriage are illegitimate because arranged marriage is not as natural as nature itself. Partners are not choosing each other by themselves; the sexual activities that proceed are basically a deal, and whatever a relationship like this produces must be called *'illegitimate'*.
The matter is not about legitimacy or illegitimacy; here the matter lies in the audacity of society, since somehow, they feel they have the authority to mark or call a baby or relationship legitimate or illegitimate. But Osho says that a relationship, love, or an offspring is a personal thing where society or legality should not interfere.
This is one of the reasons why, even today, people cry hearing Osho’s speeches.

The relationship between **Shree Ram** and **Maa Sita** is one of love; primarily, it is not a husband-wife relationship. Since it wasn’t an arranged marriage, but rather a **Swayamvar**, which loosely translates to *self-choice* or *self-selection*, even in the **Treta Yuga**, the choice of a lady was the ultimate decision for her marriage with a potential suitor. Being a *"husband"* or a *"wife"* is a particular role that society thrashes us with, with predefined dogma.

Quoting from an article on **osho.com**:

> *"An arranged marriage is determined by the parents, palm readers, astrologers, the family, and society."*

There is a great safety, security, and stability in such a marriage because, when the elders decide something, they calculate everything—every possible nuance, societal requirements, family requirements—just like shopping. The elders have their accumulated knowledge, experience, and wisdom, by which they use their shrewdness and their cunning ability to decide anything in the most perfect way possible.

Perhaps the question is not whether arranged marriage is right or wrong, legitimate or illegitimate. The real question is whether society should possess the authority to decide the most intimate dimensions of an individual’s life. Love, relationship, and the creation of life are deeply personal phenomena that cannot be measured by customs, contracts, traditions, or social approval. What Osho ultimately challenges is not merely arranged marriage, but the very assumption that society knows better than the individual. Whether one agrees with him or not, his words compel us to question what we have inherited as truth and whether habit has silently replaced freedom in matters of love.
