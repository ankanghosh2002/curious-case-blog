Astronauts miss the smell of rain because the International Space Station (ISS) which is mostly where the astronauts go to when they launch from earth, lacks organic weather, dirt, mud, living plants, bacteria, organisms or anything that shouts out life. Although they take plant seeds and grow them in the space labs and they also take microorganisms and insect to do micro g experiments but still doesn’t add up to the feeling of life or the earthly smell of rain which is called *‘**petrichor**’*. Instead, spacecraft air smells only of recycled piss, sweat, combinations of antiseptic, garbage and machines for an average of six months long mission. Returning to Earth resets their senses making ordinary earth aromas – flowers, mud, wood, air. 

Although Apollo astronauts claimed to experience an unforgettable smell after walking on Moon. When they returned to the lunar module and opened their helmets, the lunar dust attached to their suits released an odor that reminded them of *‘spent gunpowder or burnt charcoal’*. NASA scientist suspect that this is due to a chemical reaction with the dry lunar dust with oxygen and moisture present in the habitable cabin. Strangely, the scent was gone once the dust samples were brought back to Earth. It is also said that the lunar dust caused sneezing, sore throats, and eye irritation for the astronauts.

Perhaps home isn't the place we leave behind—it's the collection of ordinary things and feelings we never notice until they're gone. Sometimes, the smell of rain is enough to remind us that, in an endless universe, Earth is still the only place that truly smells alive rather than that ghostly smell of space dust and mechanic odour of the ISS.
